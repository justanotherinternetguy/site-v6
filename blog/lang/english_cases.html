Grammatical Cases in English

23/11/2023

What even is a "case"?

A case is simply a categorization that marks the semantic and syntactic function of a noun and other parts of speech related to the noun.

That was a mouthful. Let's first use English (which I hope you are familiar with) as an example of grammatical case. Then, we can observe the applications of case in other languages and conlangs.

Note that English cases are relatively simple and have been eroded down to a very minimal set through millenia of evolution from Old English. Modern English cases are strongly analytical, meaning they rely on word order and helper words to mark case instead of using inflection. A byproduct of being There are three main cases in Modern English:

  • Nominative
    • The Nominative case is used to mark the subject of a sentence. For active voice, the subject is carrying out the action of the verb:
      Caesar kicked Brutus
      Caesar.3SG.NOM kick.TR.PAST Brutus.3SG.NOM
      For passive voice the subject is being affected by the verb:
      Brutus was kicked by Caesar
      Brutus.3SG.ACC kick.TR.PST.PASS by Caesar.3SG.NOM
    • Nominative pronouns in English are:
      Singular Plural
      First Person I We
      Second Person You You + noun ("You all", "You guys", "You boys")
      Third Person He/She/It They
    • Most people call the English Nominative case the Subjective Case to make it clear that this case is relating to the subject.
  • Accusative
    • The Accusative case is used to mark the direct object of a transitive verb:
      Caesar greeted her
      Caesar.3SG.NOM greet.TR.PST her.DO.ACC
    • Transitive verb = A verb that has a direct object:
      Caesar drove the car
      Caesar.3SG.NOM drive.TR.PST the car.DO.ACC
    • Direct object = An object that is directly affected by the transitive verb.
    • Accusative pronouns in English are:
      Singular Plural
      First Person Me Us
      Second Person You You + noun ("You all", "You guys", "You boys")
      Third Person Him/Her/It Them
    • Most people call the English Accusative case the Objective Case to make it clear that this case is relating to the object.
  • Genitive
    • The Genitive case marks possession, indicating that an object is possessed/controlled by another.
    • The 's in English is a great example of inflection which marks Genitive case.
    • Of course, you can also take the analytical route with helper words such as:
      • The horse of Caesar was strong
      The.ART horse.NOM of Caesar.GEN be.PST strong.ADJ
